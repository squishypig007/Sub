Responsive Redesign



Introduction

    When thinking about good design, one of the most important things that we tend to overlook is the accessibility of the interface along with its usability. Both these areas range far beyond their spoken meanings.

    Keeping these ideas in mind while browsing the webs in my own time, I came across the website of the local "Metro Nails" salon that I frequent and chose to analyze and redesign it. I chose this website because I felt that it had meaningful content that had potential to be communicated in a more effective manner.

    Here is a screenshot of the existing Metro Nails website for reference as I analyze the problems in its interface:



Problems with Usability

    Efficiency/Usability


  • There is no clear layout making it inefficent ot scan the page
  • The navigation bar does not fit the page and is dificult to read
  • The website is not responsive and the headings wrap from right to left on the phone view
  • Images and text do not resize
  • The text is justified without hypenation, causing irregular spacing midline; this makes it inefficient to read any line
  • Updated payment, contact and health/safety related information is scattered and difficult to efficiently find

    Learnability


  • There is no clear layout making it inefficent ot scan the page
  • Lacks learning accelerators like buttons and other interactive tools
  • The external reviews do not look clickable, requiring cognitive effort to understand an unusual format of links
  • There is no natural flow of information or clear headings to learn where information is
  • Action buttons/links are generally placed below pictures to aid up-down scanning patterns; here, pictures themselves are links without any indicator

    Memorability


  • The format of this website is confusing and somewhat random, making it difficult to remember
  • No repeated elements or components, like buttons on hover, click, to aid recall
  • There are different font-styles, sizes and colors in an irregular pattern
  • The webpage is very text heavy with small images that do not fit to the screen
  • The spacing and overall layout lacks consistency and does not meet general standards, making details difficult to recall

    Conceptual model


  • The conceptual model of a website for a nail salon is likely more colorful, representing beauty and cosmetic
  • There is generally a clear "Reviews" section describing, often explicitly positive, customer experience
  • There are generally images of the overall branding or products (like nail art) on the site

Problems with Accessibility

    Some of the problems I described above are more apparent than the ones we miss the most frequently - accessibility-related issues. By using WebAIM WAIVE's guidelines, I was able to detect these problems more easibly; these include but are not limited to:

  • Missing alternative (alt) text for several images
  • Language used missing or invalid
  • Many empty headings
  • 58 instances of very low contrast
  • Fully justified text


    • I think all of the above instances are so important because the detection of these problems is required to be able to work towards a more inclusive web experience. For instance, fully justified text and empty headings leave irregular spacing making is difficult for users of assistive technologies to navigate the page.

    Not mentioning the language is part of an assumption that all users would be English-speaking which is severely problematic. Users of automatic translation of content would not be able to access the information on this place easily because of this.

    Lastly, instances of low contrast prove to be difficult to view for users with low vision or those who have some form of color-blindness.
